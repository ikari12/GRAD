%TC:break Abstract
\begin{abstract}
Meixner et al.\ (2025) proposed the Decoupling Index (DI) as one of four dimensions of exercise durability, yet no large-scale empirical validation exists. Using the FitRec dataset (253,020 workouts, 675 users), we examined construct validity, route dependence, and variance structure of the 4D framework using speed-based DI---the form deployed to the majority of real-world users. DI and the Fading Index collapsed onto a single construct ($r = -0.545$; PCA retained one factor), while the Resilience Index remained independent, reducing the framework to two or three effective dimensions. A simulation ($N = 5{,}000$; cardiac drift $= 0$) demonstrated that speed-based DI is predictable from route geometry alone ($R^2 = 0.82$): the mechanism is qualitatively obvious, but the magnitude---a 7-fold DI range with zero physiological contribution---has not been quantified and goes uncorrected in deployed platforms. Once this artifact was removed, the temporal placement of descent emerged as a physiological effect on cardiac drift (Cohen's $d = 0.69$; amplified by steepness, $r = 0.65$; absent in mountain biking; $p_{\text{FDR}} < .001$). Variance decomposition along Person\,/\,Route\,/\,Occasion axes revealed that residual variance---encompassing both true day-to-day variation and measurement error---accounted for 50--78\% of all HR-response metrics; a route-matched sensitivity analysis reduced this to 27--53\%. Gradient HR sensitivity is proposed as a candidate alternative metric (Spearman-Brown $= 0.90$; the only positive cross-validated $R^2$; speed correlation $r = +0.285$), but requires prospective validation against VO$_2$max.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}

Aerobic decoupling---the progressive rise in heart rate (HR) relative to pace or power during prolonged exercise---has been widely adopted as a field-based indicator of endurance fitness \cite{coyle2001, friel2009}. Platforms such as TrainingPeaks and Strava present the Decoupling Index (DI) to millions of athletes. On variable terrain, it is qualitatively obvious that speed---determined primarily by gradient---is a poor proxy for external workload. What has not been established is (a)~the quantitative magnitude of this bias, (b)~whether the bias dominates physiological signal in real-world data, and (c)~what genuine physiological signals emerge once the bias is removed.

Meixner et al.\ \cite{meixner2025} formalized DI within a four-dimensional (4D) durability framework (DI, Fading Index, Resilience Index, Recovery Index). Eight commentaries followed, including Millet \cite{millet2025} on DI--FI redundancy and Joyner \cite{joyner2025} on dimensional boundaries. All discussions have remained theoretical: no large-scale empirical validation exists. One likely reason is a disciplinary gap: the 4D framework assumes DI defined as $\text{HR}/\text{Power}$, while the FitRec dataset \cite{ni2018}---published in the recommender-systems community---contains HR and GPS but no power data. Yet the majority of real-world DI users compute $\text{HR}/\text{Speed}$ via consumer GPS watches. This study provides the first large-scale validation using speed-based DI, the form actually deployed to millions of users.

The reliability of field-based HR metrics has long been a concern \cite{hopkins2000, halson2014}. The IOC consensus \cite{bourdon2017} emphasized the external-load/internal-response distinction, but no study has quantified how much variance in HR-response metrics is attributable to the person, the route, or the occasion. Evidence from exercise physiology suggests that eccentric muscle contractions during downhill locomotion induce micro-damage \cite{peake2017, proske2005} affecting cardiovascular function \cite{stickford2019}. Whether the \textit{temporal placement} of descent---as opposed to its total amount---affects cardiac drift remains untested.

\subsection*{Contributions}

\textbf{C1}~Quantification of the route-geometry artifact in speed-based DI: the mechanism is obvious, but its magnitude ($R^2 = 0.82$; 7-fold DI range) has not been established. \textbf{C2}~First large-scale variance decomposition (Person/Route/Occasion) for HR-response metrics, with route-matched sensitivity analysis. \textbf{C3}~Discovery of the temporal descent-placement effect on cardiac drift---a signal obscured by the route artifact and visible only after gradient correction. \textbf{C4}~Proposal of gradient HR sensitivity as a candidate alternative metric requiring prospective validation.

\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Data}

We used the FitRec dataset \cite{ni2018} (253,020 workouts from Endomondo). Demographic information (age, training history) is unavailable; the gender variable indicates 94.0\% male, 4.8\% female, 1.2\% unknown. After filtering for concurrent HR, altitude, and speed streams with $\geq 30$ data points and non-zero altitude range ($253{,}020 \rightarrow 13{,}750$), Study~1 used 13,750 workouts from 675 users (cycling 61.8\%, running 16.9\%, mountain biking 16.7\%, other 3.6\%; median duration 181~min [IQR 132--265]; median altitude range 349~m [IQR 263--545]; median HR 135~bpm [IQR 125--145]). Studies~2--3 required $\geq 5$ repeated workouts per user, yielding 2,343 workouts from 314 users.

\subsection*{Metrics}

Six metrics per workout: (1)~DI: $(\text{HR}/\text{Speed})_{\text{H2}} / (\text{HR}/\text{Speed})_{\text{H1}}$; (2)~FI: $\text{Speed}_{\text{H2}} / \text{Speed}_{\text{H1}}$; (3)~RI: CV of DI over the last 5 workouts; and from the regression $\text{HR} \sim \text{gradient} + \text{speed} + \text{time}$: (4)~GACD ($\beta_{\text{time}}$, bpm/min); (5)~Gradient sensitivity ($\beta_{\text{grad}}$, bpm/\%); (6)~Speed sensitivity ($\beta_{\text{speed}}$, bpm/(m/s)).

\subsection*{Analysis}

\textbf{Study~1:} Bootstrap correlations ($B = 1{,}000$), PCA with parallel analysis ($N_{\text{valid}} = 9{,}917$), ICC(3,1).

\textbf{Study~2:} Ridge regression and GBT with GroupKFold CV (groups = userId). Simulation ($N = 5{,}000$; drift $= 0$). Descent-placement analysis via partial correlations, within-person correlations, steepness stratification, and dose--response curves. All exploratory $p$-values adjusted by Benjamini-Hochberg FDR.

\textbf{Study~3:} ICC (Person), GroupKFold CV $R^2$ (Route), residual (Occasion). Route-matched ICC computed for users who repeated similar routes ($\pm 20\%$ altitude range and total ascent; $n = 451$ matched pairs) to separate route variance from residual. Split-half reliability with Spearman-Brown correction; convergence analysis ($k = 2\text{--}10$).

\section*{Results}

\subsection*{Construct Validity of the 4D Framework}

DI and FI showed a strong negative correlation ($r = -0.545$, 95\% CI $[-0.56, -0.53]$; shared variance 29.7\%). PCA retained one factor (eigenvalue$_1 = 1.657$, 55.2\% variance; eigenvalue$_2 = 0.887$, below the parallel-analysis 95th-percentile threshold of 1.006; Table~\ref{tab:pca}). RI was largely independent ($|r| < 0.20$) and ReI could not be evaluated (requires longitudinal data unavailable in FitRec). All dimension ICCs fell below 0.50 (DI: 0.16; FI: 0.21; RI: 0.09).

\section*{Discussion}

\subsection*{Route Artifact: Quantification of an Expected Bias (C1)}

The mechanism by which terrain asymmetry biases speed-based DI is qualitatively obvious. What our data establish is the \textit{magnitude}: $R^2 = 0.82$ in simulation, a 7-fold DI range (0.39--2.76) with zero cardiac drift. This is not a theoretical possibility but the dominant source of DI variance on variable terrain. Friel's $< 5\%$ rule \cite{friel2009} is valid only on flat or symmetric courses---a constraint not communicated in deployed platforms.

DI--FI redundancy ($r = -0.545$; PCA retains one factor) confirms Millet's \cite{millet2025} theoretical critique with the first large-scale empirical evidence. Our DI uses $\text{HR}/\text{Speed}$, not $\text{HR}/\text{Power}$ as formally defined by Meixner et al.\ \cite{meixner2025}. The route artifact is specific to speed-based DI---power meters measure workload directly. However, this is precisely the point: the majority of real-world users---all runners, and cyclists without power meters---use speed-based DI. The contribution of C1 is not the mechanism (which is obvious) but the quantification and the warning that this dominant artifact goes uncorrected. Moreover, removing this artifact is a prerequisite for C3: the descent-placement effect is masked by the route artifact and becomes detectable only after gradient correction.

\subsection*{Descent Placement: Eccentric Damage Hypothesis (C3)}

Cardiac drift depends on the \textit{temporal placement} of descent, not its total amount ($r = +0.03$ ns vs.\ $\beta^* = +0.29$, $p_{\text{FDR}} < .001$). This is consistent with cumulative eccentric micro-damage \cite{peake2017, proske2005}. Within-person evidence is modest ($r = 0.089$), and confirmatory replication is needed.

\subsection*{Residual Variance: Day-to-Day Variation and Measurement Error (C2)}

Residual variance (Occasion) accounted for 50--78\% of HR-response metrics and was not explained by effort ($r = -0.004$ ns). This residual conflates true day-to-day variation and measurement error. Route-matched ICC analysis ($n = 451$ matched pairs) substantially increased Person ICC for all metrics: GACD ($0.22 \rightarrow 0.47$), gradient sensitivity ($0.36 \rightarrow 0.52$), speed sensitivity ($0.43 \rightarrow 0.73$), reducing the residual to 27--53\%. Even after this correction, the IOC consensus \cite{bourdon2017} principle holds quantitatively: \textit{same person, same route, 27--53\% of the HR response differs from day to day}.

\subsection*{Gradient Sensitivity: A Candidate Alternative (C4)}

Gradient sensitivity showed the most balanced---though still limited---profile: SB reaching 0.80 at $k = 6$ measurements (SB$_5 = 0.797$, SB$_6 = 0.811$), route-matched ICC $= 0.52$ (vs.\ $0.36$ overall), the only positive CV $R^2$ ($+0.089$), and speed correlation $r = +0.285$ ($p_{\text{FDR}} < .001$). However, ICC $= 0.36$ is ``poor'' \cite{hopkins2000}, and speed is a crude fitness proxy. Gradient sensitivity is proposed as a \textit{candidate} requiring prospective validation against VO$_2$max and lactate threshold with controlled sensors.

\subsection*{Limitations}

(1)~\textit{Speed-based DI}: our DI uses $\text{HR}/\text{Speed}$, not $\text{HR}/\text{Power}$. The route artifact (C1) is specific to speed-based DI; results regarding H1, H2, and H4 may differ for power-based DI. However, HR/Speed is the form deployed to the majority of real-world users. (2)~Secondary data with uncontrolled sensor quality. (3)~Residual--noise conflation: route-matched ICC partially addresses this, but full separation requires research-grade sensors. (4)~No gold standard (VO$_2$max, power). (5)~Demographics: 94\% male; age and training history unknown. (6)~Exploratory findings (E1--E3) not pre-registered. (7)~Multiple comparisons: 7/8 exploratory tests survived BH-FDR, but this does not account for broader analytic flexibility. (8)~Observational design. (9)~Single dataset. (10)~Sport bias (cycling 62\%).

% ============================================================
\section*{Conclusion}

To use aerobic decoupling for durability assessment on variable terrain, route geometry correction is essential. The bias mechanism is qualitatively obvious; our contribution is its quantification ($R^2 = 0.82$; 7-fold range) and the warning that it goes uncorrected in deployed platforms. Once the artifact is removed, the temporal placement of descent emerges as a physiological effect ($d = 0.69$), though within-person evidence is modest and replication is needed. Residual variance accounts for 27--53\% of HR-response metrics after route matching, providing a quantitative upper bound on the external-load/internal-response dissociation. Gradient sensitivity (SB$_6 = 0.811$; route-matched ICC $= 0.52$; speed $r = +0.285$) is the most promising candidate, but ICC $= 0.36$ limits between-person comparison and prospective validation against VO$_2$max is essential.
