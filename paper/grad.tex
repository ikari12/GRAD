% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
\citet{meixner2025} proposed the Decoupling Index (DI) as one of four dimensions of exercise durability, generating eight theoretical responses. However, no large-scale empirical validation exists. Using the FitRec dataset \citep{ni2018} (253,020 workouts, 675 users), we examined the construct validity, route dependence, and variance structure of the 4D framework. DI and the Fading Index collapsed onto a single construct ($r = -0.60$; PCA retained one factor), indicating that the 4D framework is effectively two-dimensional. A simulation ($N = 5{,}000$; cardiac drift $= 0$) demonstrated that DI on variable terrain is predictable from route geometry alone ($R^2 = 0.82$), constituting a mathematical artifact independent of physiology (real-data $R^2 = 0.60$). Once this artifact was removed, a genuine physiological effect emerged: the temporal placement of descent increased cardiac drift (Cohen's $d = 0.69$; amplified by steepness, $r = 0.65$; absent in mountain biking). Variance decomposition along Person / Route / Occasion axes revealed that day-to-day variation (Occasion) accounted for 50--78\% of variance in all HR-response metrics, unexplained by effort ($r = -0.004$, ns). Gradient HR sensitivity, proposed as an alternative durability metric, showed a Spearman-Brown reliability of 0.90 (converging within five measurements), the only positive cross-validated $R^2$ ($+0.089$), and a significant correlation with average speed ($r = +0.33$). When using DI for durability assessment on variable terrain, route correction and integration of daily readiness information are essential.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} aerobic decoupling, durability, cardiac drift, heart rate, gradient sensitivity, measurement artifact, field testing
\end{abstract}

% INTRODUCTION

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Background}

Aerobic decoupling---the progressive rise in heart rate (HR) relative to pace or power during prolonged exercise---has been widely adopted as a field-based indicator of endurance fitness \citep{coyle2001, friel2009}. Platforms such as TrainingPeaks and Strava present the Decoupling Index (DI) to millions of recreational and competitive athletes, typically applying a $<5\%$ threshold to indicate adequate aerobic base fitness.

\citet{meixner2025} formalized DI within a four-dimensional (4D) durability framework comprising the Decoupling Index (DI), Fading Index (FI), Resilience Index (RI), and Recovery Index (ReI). This proposal generated eight commentaries, including \citet{millet2025}, who argued that DI and FI are theoretically redundant, and \citet{joyner2025}, who questioned the conceptual boundaries between dimensions. Critically, all discussions have remained theoretical: no large-scale empirical validation of the 4D framework exists.

The reliability of field-based HR metrics has long been a concern. \citet{hopkins2000} established criteria for measurement reliability in sport, and \citet{halson2014} documented the influence of diurnal and day-to-day variation on training-load metrics. The IOC consensus statement \citep{bourdon2017} emphasized the distinction between external load and internal response, but the quantitative decomposition of variance sources (Person, Route, Occasion) has not been performed.

Meanwhile, evidence from exercise physiology suggests that eccentric muscle contractions during downhill locomotion induce micro-damage and inflammatory responses \citep{peake2017, proske2005}, and \citet{stickford2019} reported that downhill running increases subsequent cardiac drift. Whether the \textit{temporal placement} of descent---as opposed to its total amount---systematically affects cardiac drift in field conditions remains untested.

\subsection{Research Questions and Hypotheses}

The central research question is: \textit{Is aerobic decoupling a valid indicator of cardiovascular fitness on variable terrain?}

\subsection{Contributions}

This study makes four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[\textbf{C1}] Demonstration that aerobic decoupling on variable terrain is a mathematical artifact of route geometry (methodological warning).
  \item[\textbf{C2}] First large-scale quantification of Person / Route / Occasion variance in HR-response metrics (descriptive).
  \item[\textbf{C3}] Discovery of the temporal descent-placement effect on cardiac drift (exploratory finding).
  \item[\textbf{C4}] Proposal of gradient HR sensitivity as a reliable alternative durability metric (metric proposal).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Data}

We used the FitRec dataset \citep{ni2018}, comprising 253,020 workout recordings from the Endomondo platform. Inclusion criteria required concurrent HR, altitude, and speed time-series streams with $\geq 30$ data points. After filtering, Study~1 used 13,750 workouts from 675 users, and Studies~2--3 used 2,343 workouts from 288 users with $\geq 5$ repeated workouts per user.

\subsection{Metric Operationalization}

Six metrics were computed for each workout (Table~\ref{tab:metrics}).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering

\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}

Metric & Computation \\
\midrule

\subsection{Analytical Approach}

\textbf{Study~1 (Construct Validity)} examined inter-metric correlations via bootstrap Pearson correlations ($B = 1{,}000$), dimensionality via PCA with parallel analysis, and test--retest reliability via ICC(3,1).

\textbf{Study~2 (Artifact Detection)} predicted DI from route features using Ridge regression and gradient-boosted trees (GBT) with GroupKFold cross-validation (groups = userId). A simulation ($N = 5{,}000$; cardiac drift $= 0$) generated synthetic HR data to isolate the structural artifact. The descent-placement effect was assessed using partial correlations, within-person analyses, stratified analyses by steepness, and dose--response curves.

\textbf{Study~3 (Variance Decomposition)} decomposed metric variance into Person (ICC), Route (GroupKFold CV~$R^2$), and Occasion (residual) components, with bootstrap 95\% CIs ($B = 1{,}000$). Reliability was assessed via split-half correlations with Spearman-Brown correction and convergence analysis (2--10 measurements).

All cross-validations used \texttt{GroupKFold(groups=userId)} to prevent data leakage from repeated measures of the same user appearing in both training and test sets.

\subsection{Simulation Proof of Artifact (Figure~\ref{fig:simulation})}

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering

\caption{DI as a function of gradient asymmetry in $N = 5{,}000$ synthetic workouts with zero cardiac drift. Route types are distinguished by marker shape and color. The correlation $r = -0.88$ ($p < .001$) demonstrates that DI is determined by route geometry, not fatigue.}

With cardiac drift set to exactly zero, DI was predictable from route features alone (CV~$R^2 = 0.82$, SD~$= 0.006$). Front-climb routes produced $\text{DI} = 0.39 \pm 0.10$ (false ``high fitness''), back-climb routes produced $\text{DI} = 2.76 \pm 0.82$ (false ``fatigue''), and symmetric routes produced $\text{DI} = 1.00 \pm 0.10$ (correct). This confirms H3: DI values on variable terrain are a mathematical artifact of the formula's sensitivity to gradient asymmetry.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Aerobic Decoupling Requires Route Correction on Variable Terrain (C1)}

Route predictability of DI was confirmed in both simulation ($R^2 = 0.82$) and real data ($R^2 = 0.60$ for hilly subsets). This predictability persists even when cardiac drift is exactly zero (Figure~\ref{fig:simulation}). The mechanism is straightforward: in $\text{DI} = (\text{HR}/\text{Speed})_{\text{H2}} / (\text{HR}/\text{Speed})_{\text{H1}}$, speed is strongly determined by terrain. On front-climb routes (ascent first, descent second), H1 speed is low and H2 speed is high, producing $\text{DI} < 1$ (``high fitness'') even with no change in HR.

\textbf{Practical implication:} The $\text{Pa:HR} < 5\%$ rule \citep{friel2009} is valid only on flat or symmetric courses. This constraint is not made explicit in platforms such as TrainingPeaks and Strava.

Regarding the 4D framework \citep{meixner2025}, DI--FI redundancy ($r = -0.60$) and RI independence are consistent with \citeauthor{millet2025}'s (\citeyear{millet2025}) theoretical critique, and this study provides the first large-scale empirical confirmation ($N = 13{,}750$). PCA retention of a single factor supports a 4D~$\rightarrow$~2D reduction.

\subsection{Descent Placement Effect: Temporal Eccentric Damage Hypothesis (C3)}

Table~\ref{tab:descent} demonstrates that the increase in cardiac drift depends on the \textit{temporal placement} of descent, not its total amount (total descent: $r = +0.03$ ns; descent-front ratio: $\beta^* = +0.29^{***}$). This asymmetry is consistent with the cumulative micro-damage hypothesis from eccentric muscle contractions \citep{peake2017, proske2005}.

Three lines of evidence support this interpretation: (1)~the effect is amplified in moderately steep terrain ($r = +0.65$) compared to gentle terrain ($r = +0.18$), consistent with greater eccentric loads on steeper descents; (2)~a threshold effect is observed in the top decile of descent-front ratio (GACD jumps to 0.086); and (3)~the effect disappears in mountain biking ($r = +0.01$ ns), plausibly because suspension and seated posture absorb eccentric impact.

\subsection{Occasion Dominates: The Invisibility of Daily Condition (C2)}

The most practically important finding is that Occasion (day-to-day variation) accounted for the majority of variance in all HR-response metrics: GACD 78.1\%, gradient sensitivity 58.5\%, speed sensitivity 56.9\%.

This Occasion variance was \textit{not} explained by effort ($r = -0.004$ ns). Rather, unmeasured factors---sleep, hydration, temperature, stress, prior-day training load---dominate HR responses. The IOC consensus \citep{bourdon2017} conceptually argued that external load $\neq$ internal response, but this study provides the first quantification: ``Even when the same person runs the same route, 50--78\% of the HR response differs from day to day.''

\textbf{Practical implication:} When wearable applications estimate fitness from a single HR response, more than half of the estimate is day-to-day noise. Route correction and integration of readiness information are essential.

\subsection{Gradient Sensitivity: Limited but Most Promising Alternative (C4)}

Gradient HR sensitivity showed the most balanced profile among the four metrics:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Reliability:} SB $= 0.90$ (split-half $r$ reaches 0.80 within five measurements).
  \item \textbf{Route relationship:} GroupKFold CV~$R^2 = +0.089$ (the only positive CV~$R^2$).
  \item \textbf{External validity:} Correlation with average speed $r = +0.33^{**}$ (fitter individuals show a blunted HR response to gradient).
  \item \textbf{Temporal stability:} Early-third vs.\ late-third correlation $r = 0.77$.
\end{itemize}

However, ICC $= 0.36$ is ``poor'' by \citeauthor{hopkins2000}'s (\citeyear{hopkins2000}) criteria, and Occasion still accounts for 58.5\%. Gradient sensitivity outperforms existing metrics but is \textit{insufficient for between-person comparison alone}. Prospective studies correlating gradient sensitivity with VO$_2$max and lactate threshold are needed.

% LIMITATIONS
% ============================================================
\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Secondary data.} FitRec was not collected for research purposes; HR sensor quality, placement, and calibration are uncontrolled. Noise may underestimate reliability metrics.
  \item \textbf{No gold standard.} Without VO$_2$max, lactate threshold, or power-meter data, construct validity of gradient sensitivity rests on indirect evidence.
  \item \textbf{Missing demographics.} Age, sex, and training history are unknown and may confound both Person and Occasion variance.
  \item \textbf{Exploratory findings.} E1 (descent placement), E2 (alternative metrics), and E3 (ICC--SB discrepancy) were not pre-registered and require confirmatory replication.
  \item \textbf{Causal inference.} The descent-placement effect is observational; reverse causality cannot be excluded.
  \item \textbf{Occasion composition unknown.} Day-to-day variation accounts for 50--78\% of variance, but its constituents (sleep, temperature, hydration, stress) cannot be identified in this dataset.
  \item \textbf{Single dataset.} Only FitRec was used; replication in different user populations is needed.
  \item \textbf{Sport bias.} Cycling 62\%, running 17\%, mountain biking 17\%, hiking 1\%. Generalization to hiking and mountaineering requires caution.
\end{enumerate}

% ============================================================
% CONCLUSION
% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}

To use aerobic decoupling for durability assessment on variable terrain, route geometry correction is essential. Route prediction $R^2 = 0.82$ (simulation) / $0.60$ (real data) demonstrates that DI values are largely determined by the formula's sensitivity to terrain asymmetry. Once this artifact is appropriately removed, the temporal placement of descent emerges as a genuine physiological effect on cardiac drift ($d = 0.69$). Day-to-day variation accounts for 50--78\% of HR-response variance, demonstrating that ``even when the same person runs the same route, the HR response differs every time.'' Gradient sensitivity (SB~$= 0.90$; converging within five measurements; speed correlation $r = +0.33$) is the most promising alternative, but ICC~$= 0.36$ indicates limitations for between-person comparison, and integration of route correction with daily readiness information remains a key direction for future work.
