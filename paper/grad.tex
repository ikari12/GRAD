\title{Beyond Aerobic Decoupling: Route Artifacts, Day-to-Day Noise, and Gradient Sensitivity as an Alternative Durability Metric in 253K Workouts}
\shorttitle{GRAD Study}

%TC:break Abstract
\begin{abstract}
Meixner et al.\ (2025) proposed the Decoupling Index (DI) as one of four dimensions of exercise durability, generating eight theoretical responses, yet no large-scale empirical validation exists. Using the FitRec dataset (253,020 workouts, 675 users), we examined the construct validity, route dependence, and variance structure of the 4D framework. DI and the Fading Index collapsed onto a single construct ($r = -0.60$; PCA retained one factor), indicating that the framework is effectively two-dimensional. A simulation ($N = 5{,}000$; cardiac drift $= 0$) demonstrated that DI on variable terrain is predictable from route geometry alone ($R^2 = 0.82$), constituting a mathematical artifact independent of physiology (real-data $R^2 = 0.60$). Once this artifact was removed, the temporal placement of descent emerged as a genuine physiological effect on cardiac drift (Cohen's $d = 0.69$; amplified by steepness, $r = 0.65$; absent in mountain biking). Variance decomposition along Person\,/\,Route\,/\,Occasion axes revealed that day-to-day variation accounted for 50--78\% of all HR-response metrics, unexplained by effort ($r = -0.004$, ns). Gradient HR sensitivity, proposed as an alternative durability metric, showed a Spearman-Brown reliability of 0.90 (converging within five measurements), the only positive cross-validated $R^2$ ($+0.089$), and a significant speed correlation ($r = +0.33$). When using DI for durability assessment on variable terrain, route correction and integration of daily readiness information are essential.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}

Aerobic decoupling---the progressive rise in heart rate (HR) relative to pace or power during prolonged exercise---has been widely adopted as a field-based indicator of endurance fitness \cite{coyle2001, friel2009}. Platforms such as TrainingPeaks and Strava present the Decoupling Index (DI) to millions of recreational and competitive athletes, typically applying a $<5\%$ threshold to indicate adequate aerobic base fitness.

Meixner et al.\ \cite{meixner2025} formalized DI within a four-dimensional (4D) durability framework comprising the Decoupling Index (DI), Fading Index (FI), Resilience Index (RI), and Recovery Index (ReI). This proposal generated eight commentaries, including Millet \cite{millet2025}, who argued that DI and FI are theoretically redundant, and Joyner \cite{joyner2025}, who questioned the conceptual boundaries between dimensions. Critically, \textit{all discussions have remained theoretical}: no large-scale empirical validation of the 4D framework exists.

The reliability of field-based HR metrics has long been a concern. Hopkins \cite{hopkins2000} established criteria for measurement reliability in sport, and Halson \cite{halson2014} documented the influence of diurnal and day-to-day variation on training-load metrics. The IOC consensus statement \cite{bourdon2017} emphasized the distinction between external load and internal response, but the quantitative decomposition of variance sources (Person, Route, Occasion) has not been performed.

Meanwhile, evidence from exercise physiology suggests that eccentric muscle contractions during downhill locomotion induce micro-damage and inflammatory responses \cite{peake2017, proske2005}, and Stickford et al.\ \cite{stickford2019} reported that downhill running increases subsequent cardiac drift. Whether the \textit{temporal placement} of descent---as opposed to its total amount---systematically affects cardiac drift in field conditions remains untested.

\subsection*{Research Question}

The central research question is: \textit{Is aerobic decoupling a valid indicator of cardiovascular fitness on variable terrain?}

We tested four confirmatory hypotheses---DI--FI redundancy (H1), poor ICC for all dimensions (H2), DI route prediction vanishing after gradient correction (H3), and Occasion $> 50\%$ of variance (H4)---and three exploratory hypotheses concerning descent placement (E1), alternative metrics (E2), and the ICC--SB discrepancy (E3).

\subsection*{Contributions}

This study makes four contributions: \textbf{C1}~Demonstration that aerobic decoupling on variable terrain is a mathematical artifact of route geometry; \textbf{C2}~First large-scale quantification of Person\,/\,Route\,/\,Occasion variance in HR-response metrics; \textbf{C3}~Discovery of the temporal descent-placement effect on cardiac drift; \textbf{C4}~Proposal of gradient HR sensitivity as a reliable alternative durability metric.

\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Data}

We used the FitRec dataset \cite{ni2018}, comprising 253,020 workout recordings from the Endomondo platform. Inclusion criteria required concurrent HR, altitude, and speed time-series with $\geq 30$ data points. After filtering, Study~1 used 13,750 workouts from 675 users, and Studies~2--3 used 2,343 workouts from 288 users with $\geq 5$ repeated workouts per user.

\subsection*{Metric Operationalization}

Six metrics were computed for each workout: (1)~DI (naive): $(\text{HR}/\text{Speed})_{\text{H2}} / (\text{HR}/\text{Speed})_{\text{H1}}$; (2)~FI: $\text{Speed}_{\text{H2}} / \text{Speed}_{\text{H1}}$; (3)~RI: CV of DI over the last 5 workouts; (4)~GACD: coefficient of time from $\text{HR} \sim \text{gradient} + \text{speed} + \text{time}$ (bpm/min); (5)~Gradient sensitivity: coefficient of gradient from the same regression (bpm/1\% grade); (6)~Speed sensitivity: coefficient of speed (bpm/(m/s)).

\subsection*{Analytical Approach}

\textbf{Study~1 (Construct Validity):} Bootstrap Pearson correlations ($B = 1{,}000$), PCA with parallel analysis, and ICC(3,1).

\textbf{Study~2 (Artifact Detection):} Ridge regression and gradient-boosted trees with GroupKFold CV (groups = userId). Simulation ($N = 5{,}000$; cardiac drift $= 0$). Descent-placement effect via partial correlations, within-person analyses, steepness stratification, and dose--response curves.

\textbf{Study~3 (Variance Decomposition):} ICC (Person), GroupKFold CV $R^2$ (Route), and residual (Occasion), with bootstrap 95\% CIs. Split-half with Spearman-Brown correction and convergence analysis (2--10 measurements).

All cross-validations used \texttt{GroupKFold(groups=userId)} to prevent data leakage.

\section*{Discussion}

\subsection*{DI Requires Route Correction on Variable Terrain (C1)}

Route predictability of DI was confirmed in both simulation ($R^2 = 0.82$) and real data ($R^2 = 0.60$). The mechanism is straightforward: speed is terrain-dependent, so $\text{DI} = (\text{HR}/\text{Speed})_{\text{H2}} / (\text{HR}/\text{Speed})_{\text{H1}}$ systematically deviates from 1.0 on asymmetric routes even with constant cardiac drift. The Pa:HR $< 5\%$ rule \cite{friel2009} is valid only on flat or symmetric courses---a constraint not currently made explicit in platforms such as TrainingPeaks and Strava.

Regarding the 4D framework \cite{meixner2025}, DI--FI redundancy ($r = -0.60$) and RI independence confirm Millet's \cite{millet2025} theoretical critique with the first large-scale empirical data ($N = 13{,}750$). PCA retention of one factor supports a 4D $\rightarrow$ 2D reduction.

\subsection*{Descent Placement: Eccentric Damage Hypothesis (C3)}

Cardiac drift increases depend on the \textit{temporal placement} of descent, not its total amount ($r = +0.03$ ns vs.\ $\beta^* = +0.29^{***}$). This is consistent with cumulative micro-damage from eccentric contractions \cite{peake2017, proske2005}. The effect is amplified on steeper terrain, shows a dose--response threshold, and disappears in mountain biking---all consistent with an eccentric-load mechanism.

\subsection*{Occasion Dominates: Invisibility of Daily Condition (C2)}

Day-to-day variation accounted for 50--78\% of HR-response variance and was not explained by effort ($r = -0.004$ ns). Unmeasured factors---sleep, hydration, temperature, stress---dominate HR responses. The IOC consensus \cite{bourdon2017} conceptually argued that external load $\neq$ internal response; this study provides the first quantification: \textit{even when the same person runs the same route, 50--78\% of the HR response differs from day to day.} Route correction and readiness integration are essential for any single-session fitness estimate.

\subsection*{Gradient Sensitivity: Most Promising Alternative (C4)}

Gradient HR sensitivity showed the most balanced profile: SB $= 0.90$ (five measurements), the only positive CV $R^2$ ($+0.089$), speed correlation $r = +0.33^{**}$, and temporal stability $r = 0.77$. However, ICC $= 0.36$ is ``poor'' \cite{hopkins2000}, and Occasion still accounts for 58.5\%. Gradient sensitivity outperforms existing metrics but is insufficient for between-person comparison alone. Prospective studies with VO$_2$max and lactate threshold are needed.

\subsection*{Limitations}

(1)~Secondary data: HR sensor quality uncontrolled. (2)~No gold standard (VO$_2$max, power). (3)~Missing demographics (age, sex, training history). (4)~Exploratory findings (E1--E3) require confirmatory replication. (5)~Observational design precludes causal inference. (6)~Occasion composition (sleep, temperature, etc.) cannot be identified. (7)~Single dataset. (8)~Sport bias (cycling 62\%, hiking 1\%).

% ============================================================
\section*{Conclusion}

To use aerobic decoupling for durability assessment on variable terrain, route geometry correction is essential. Route prediction $R^2 = 0.82$ (simulation)\,/\,$0.60$ (real data) demonstrates that DI values are largely determined by terrain asymmetry. Once this artifact is appropriately removed, the temporal placement of descent emerges as a genuine physiological effect ($d = 0.69$). Day-to-day variation accounts for 50--78\% of HR-response variance, demonstrating that the same person running the same route produces a different HR response every time. Gradient sensitivity (SB $= 0.90$; speed correlation $r = +0.33$) is the most promising alternative, but ICC $= 0.36$ indicates limitations for between-person comparison, and integration with daily readiness information remains a key direction for future work.
