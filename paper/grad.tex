%TC:break Abstract
\begin{abstract}
Meixner et al.\ (2025) proposed the Decoupling Index (DI) as one of four dimensions of exercise durability, yet no large-scale empirical validation exists. Using the FitRec dataset (253,020 workouts, 675 users), we examined construct validity, route dependence, and variance structure of the 4D framework using speed-based DI---the form deployed to the majority of real-world users. DI and the Fading Index collapsed onto a single construct ($r = -0.60$; PCA retained one factor with eigenvalue $= 1.66$), reducing the framework to two or three effective dimensions. A simulation ($N = 5{,}000$; cardiac drift $= 0$) confirmed that speed-based DI is predictable from route geometry alone (CV $R^2 = 0.82$; DI range 0.39--2.76). After gradient correction, variance decomposition along Person\,/\,Route\,/\,Occasion axes revealed that residual variance dominated all HR-response metrics (57--83\%); route-matched sensitivity analysis reduced this to 27--53\% but the core finding persists: the same person on the same route produces a different HR response every session. Split-half convergence analysis showed that gradient sensitivity requires $\geq 6$ sessions to reach adequate reliability (SB $\geq 0.80$), while gradient-adjusted cardiac drift never reaches this threshold even at 10 sessions. The temporal placement of descent showed a between-person association with cardiac drift ($\beta^* = +0.29$, $p < .001$), but within-person evidence was modest ($r = 0.089$) and confirmatory replication is needed. Gradient HR sensitivity is proposed as a candidate alternative metric (SB $= 0.90$; the only positive cross-validated $R^2$; speed $r = +0.33$), but ICC $= 0.36$ precludes between-person comparison from a single measurement.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}

Aerobic decoupling---the progressive rise in heart rate (HR) relative to pace or power during prolonged exercise---has been widely adopted as a field-based indicator of endurance fitness \cite{coyle2001, friel2009}. Platforms such as TrainingPeaks and Strava present the Decoupling Index (DI) to millions of athletes, applying Friel's $<5\%$ threshold to indicate adequate aerobic base. This threshold has never been empirically validated in a peer-reviewed study.

On variable terrain, it is qualitatively obvious that speed---determined primarily by gradient---is a poor proxy for external workload, and therefore that speed-based DI will be biased by route geometry. What has not been established is (a)~the magnitude of this bias, (b)~whether the bias dominates physiological signal in real-world data, and (c)~what remains once the bias is removed.

Meixner et al.\ \cite{meixner2025} formalized DI within a four-dimensional durability framework (DI, Fading Index, Resilience Index, Recovery Index). Eight commentaries followed, including Millet \cite{millet2025} on DI--FI redundancy. All discussions have remained theoretical. One likely reason is a disciplinary gap: the 4D framework defines DI as $\text{HR}/\text{Power}$, while the FitRec dataset \cite{ni2018}---published in the recommender-systems community---contains HR and GPS but no power data. Yet the majority of real-world users compute $\text{HR}/\text{Speed}$ via consumer GPS watches. This study provides the first large-scale validation using speed-based DI, the form actually deployed to millions of users.

The reliability of field-based HR metrics has long been a concern \cite{hopkins2000, halson2014}. The IOC consensus \cite{bourdon2017} emphasized the external-load/internal-response distinction, but no study has quantified the variance attributable to person, route, and occasion in HR-response metrics.

\subsection*{Research Question}

\textit{Is aerobic decoupling a valid indicator of cardiovascular fitness on variable terrain?}

We tested four confirmatory hypotheses:
\begin{itemize}
  \item[\textbf{H1}] DI and FI collapse onto a single construct.
  \item[\textbf{H2}] All durability dimension ICCs fall below 0.50.
  \item[\textbf{H3}] DI route predictability vanishes after gradient correction.
  \item[\textbf{H4}] Occasion accounts for $>50\%$ of HR-response variance.
\end{itemize}
And three exploratory hypotheses:
\begin{itemize}
  \item[\textbf{E1}] Temporal placement of descent affects cardiac drift.
  \item[\textbf{E2}] A more stable HR parameter than DI exists.
  \item[\textbf{E3}] ICC $\ll$ split-half reliability (rank-order stability $>$ absolute consistency).
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Contributions}

\textbf{C1}~Quantification of the route-geometry artifact in speed-based DI: the mechanism is obvious, but the magnitude ($R^2 = 0.82$; 7-fold range) has not been established. \textbf{C2}~First large-scale variance decomposition (Person/Route/Occasion) for HR-response metrics, with route-matched sensitivity analysis: the same person on the same route shows 27--53\% day-to-day variation. \textbf{C3}~A between-person association between descent placement and cardiac drift, with modest within-person support. \textbf{C4}~Proposal of gradient HR sensitivity as a candidate alternative metric with convergence analysis ($\geq 6$ sessions for SB $\geq 0.80$).

% ============================================================
\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Data}

We used the FitRec dataset \cite{ni2018} (253,020 workouts from Endomondo). The gender variable indicates 94.0\% male, 4.8\% female, 1.2\% unknown; age and training history are unavailable. After filtering for concurrent HR, altitude, and speed streams with $\geq 30$ data points and non-zero altitude range ($253{,}020 \rightarrow 13{,}750$), Study~1 used 13,750 workouts from 675 users (cycling 61.8\%, running 16.9\%, mountain biking 16.7\%, other 3.6\%; median duration 181~min [IQR 132--265]; median altitude range 349~m [IQR 263--545]; median HR 135~bpm [IQR 125--145]). Studies~2--3 required $\geq 5$ repeated workouts per user, yielding 2,343 workouts from 314 users.

\subsection*{Metrics}

Six metrics per workout: (1)~DI: $(\text{HR}/\text{Speed})_{\text{H2}} / (\text{HR}/\text{Speed})_{\text{H1}}$; (2)~FI: $\text{Speed}_{\text{H2}} / \text{Speed}_{\text{H1}}$; (3)~RI: CV of DI over the last 5 workouts; and from the within-workout regression $\text{HR} \sim \text{gradient} + \text{speed} + \text{time}$: (4)~GACD ($\beta_{\text{time}}$, bpm/min); (5)~Gradient sensitivity ($\beta_{\text{grad}}$, bpm/\%); (6)~Speed sensitivity ($\beta_{\text{speed}}$, bpm/(m/s)).

\subsection*{Analysis}

\textbf{Study~1 (Construct Validity, $N = 13{,}750$):} Bootstrap correlations ($B = 1{,}000$), PCA with parallel analysis ($N_{\text{valid}} = 9{,}917$), ICC(3,1).

\textbf{Study~2 (Artifact Detection, $N = 2{,}343$ real $+ 5{,}000$ simulated):} Ridge regression and GBT with GroupKFold CV (groups = userId). Simulation ($N = 5{,}000$; drift $= 0$). Descent-placement analysis via partial correlations, within-person correlations, steepness stratification, and dose--response curves. All exploratory $p$-values adjusted by Benjamini-Hochberg FDR.

\textbf{Study~3 (Variance Decomposition, $N = 2{,}343$):} ICC (Person), GroupKFold CV $R^2$ (Route), residual (Occasion). Route-matched ICC for users who repeated similar routes ($\pm 20\%$ altitude range and total ascent; $n = 451$ matched pairs). Split-half reliability with Spearman-Brown correction; convergence analysis ($k = 2\text{--}10$).

\section*{Results}

\subsection*{Construct Validity (H1, H2)}

DI and FI showed $r = -0.60$ (95\% CI $[-0.69, -0.49]$; bootstrap $N = 13{,}750$). PCA on standardized DI, FI, RI ($N = 9{,}917$) retained one factor (eigenvalue$_1 = 1.66$, 55.2\%; eigenvalue$_2 = 0.89$, below the parallel-analysis 95th-percentile threshold; Table~\ref{tab:pca}). RI was largely independent (DI--RI: $r = -0.19$; FI--RI: $r = +0.19$). All ICCs fell below 0.50: DI $= 0.16$, FI $= 0.08$, RI $= 0.10$. H1 and H2 are confirmed.

With cardiac drift set to exactly zero, DI was predictable from route features alone (CV $R^2 = 0.82$). In real data, CV $R^2 = 0.58$ (all routes) and $0.60$ (hilly subset). After gradient correction (GACD), route predictability vanished (CV $R^2 = -0.03$). H3 is confirmed.

Occasion dominated all metrics (57--83\%), confirming H4. Gradient sensitivity outperformed DI across all criteria (E2). The ICC--SB discrepancy was largest for DI (0.16 vs.\ 0.63), confirming E3: rank-order consistency is substantially higher than absolute consistency when Occasion variance is large.

Route-matched ICC ($n = 451$ matched pairs) substantially increased Person estimates for all metrics: GACD ($0.22 \rightarrow 0.47$), gradient sensitivity ($0.36 \rightarrow 0.52$), speed sensitivity ($0.43 \rightarrow 0.73$). This indicates that 16--30 percentage points of variance previously attributed to Occasion is actually route-related, reducing the residual to 27--53\%.

Convergence analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:framework}) showed that gradient sensitivity reached SB $\geq 0.80$ at $k = 6$ sessions (SB$_5 = 0.80$, SB$_{10} = 0.88$), speed sensitivity at $k = 3$ (SB$_3 = 0.81$), while GACD never reached 0.80 even at $k = 10$ (SB$_{10} = 0.71$).

A between-person association was observed between descent-front ratio and cardiac drift ($\beta^* = +0.29$, $p_{\text{FDR}} < .001$), moderated by steepness ($r = +0.53$ on moderate terrain vs.\ $+0.10$ on gentle terrain) and absent in mountain biking. Within-person evidence was modest: 66/108 users (61\%) showed positive correlations, with a small mean $r = +0.089$. Seven of eight exploratory tests survived FDR correction. The between-person pattern is consistent with an eccentric-damage mechanism \cite{peake2017, proske2005}, but the weak within-person support precludes causal conclusions.

\section*{Discussion}

\subsection*{Route Artifact: Quantification of an Expected Bias (C1)}

The mechanism by which terrain asymmetry biases speed-based DI is qualitatively obvious. Our contribution is the quantification: $R^2 = 0.82$ in simulation, a 7-fold DI range with zero cardiac drift. In real data, route features account for more DI variance than any physiological process ($R^2 = 0.60$). Friel's $< 5\%$ rule \cite{friel2009}---itself never empirically validated---is applicable only on flat or symmetric courses, a constraint not communicated in deployed platforms.

Our DI uses $\text{HR}/\text{Speed}$, not $\text{HR}/\text{Power}$ as formally defined by Meixner et al.\ \cite{meixner2025}. The route artifact is specific to speed-based DI; power meters measure workload directly. However, $\text{HR}/\text{Speed}$ is the form used by all runners and cyclists without power meters---the majority of real-world users.

\subsection*{Occasion Dominates: The Invisibility of Daily Condition (C2)}

The central finding of this study is that day-to-day variation---not person, not route---dominates HR-response metrics. Even after gradient correction (GACD), Occasion accounted for 78\% of variance. Even after route matching ($\pm 20\%$ altitude and ascent), residual variance remained 27--53\%. This is not explained by effort intensity ($r = -0.004$, ns).

This result provides the first quantitative evidence for the IOC consensus \cite{bourdon2017} principle that external load $\neq$ internal response. Unmeasured factors---sleep, hydration, temperature, psychological stress \cite{halson2014}---are the primary determinants of HR responses in the field.

The practical consequence is that \textit{no single-session HR-response metric can reliably assess fitness}. Our convergence analysis provides specific guidance: gradient sensitivity requires $\geq 6$ sessions (SB $\geq 0.80$), speed sensitivity $\geq 3$, while GACD never reaches adequate reliability. Integration of daily readiness indicators (resting HR, HRV, sleep metrics) is essential for interpreting any single session.

\subsection*{DI--FI Redundancy Confirms Millet's Critique (H1)}

DI and FI collapse onto one PCA factor ($r = -0.60$; eigenvalue$_1 = 1.66$, PC2 below parallel threshold). Combined with poor ICCs ($< 0.21$), the 4D framework reduces to two or three effective dimensions when tested empirically. Whether this extends to power-based DI remains an open question.

\subsection*{Descent Placement: Suggestive but Unconfirmed (C3)}

Descent placement showed a between-person association with cardiac drift ($\beta^* = +0.29$), moderated by steepness and absent in mountain biking---a pattern consistent with eccentric muscle damage \cite{peake2017}. However, the within-person effect was small ($r = 0.089$), meaning individual-level causality cannot be established from these data. This finding is exploratory and requires confirmation in controlled designs.

\subsection*{Gradient Sensitivity: A Candidate with Known Limitations (C4)}

Gradient sensitivity showed the most balanced profile: SB reaching $0.80$ at $k = 6$, route-matched ICC $= 0.52$, the only positive CV $R^2$ ($+0.089$), and speed correlation $r = +0.33$ ($p < .001$). However, ICC $= 0.36$ is ``poor'' by Hopkins' \cite{hopkins2000} criteria, and speed is a crude fitness proxy. Gradient sensitivity is proposed as a \textit{candidate} requiring prospective validation against VO$_2$max and lactate threshold.

\subsection*{Limitations}

(1)~\textit{Speed-based DI}: our DI uses $\text{HR}/\text{Speed}$, not $\text{HR}/\text{Power}$. The route artifact (C1) is specific to speed-based DI; H1, H2, H4 may differ for power-based DI. (2)~Secondary data with uncontrolled sensor quality. (3)~Residual--noise conflation: route-matched ICC partially addresses this, but full separation requires research-grade sensors. (4)~No gold standard (VO$_2$max, power). (5)~Demographics: 94\% male; age and training history unknown. (6)~Exploratory findings not pre-registered. (7)~Multiple comparisons: 7/8 tests survived BH-FDR, but does not account for broader analytic flexibility. (8)~Observational design. (9)~Single dataset. (10)~Sport bias (cycling 62\%).

% ============================================================
\section*{Conclusion}

Speed-based aerobic decoupling on variable terrain is dominated by route geometry ($R^2 = 0.82$) and day-to-day variation (27--53\% after route matching). Person-level physiological signal accounts for only 22--43\% of variance. Single-session HR-response metrics cannot reliably assess fitness; gradient sensitivity requires $\geq 6$ sessions to reach adequate reliability, and gradient-adjusted cardiac drift never does. The descent-placement effect is suggestive but unconfirmed within persons. When HR-based durability metrics are used in the field, route correction and multi-session aggregation are essential; integration with daily readiness indicators remains a key direction.
